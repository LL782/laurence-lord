National Liberation Struggles

Fought to free countries and people from control by colonial powers. Fanon emphasises that such struggles are military, but also political and cultural. The Liberation is from all external domination, including direct occupation, puppets and settler regimes. The struggle in Algeria was a key early example and the film "The Battle of Algiers" a seminal film document of such struggles.



Negroism

The concept of 'negritude' developed in the 1930s in response to European racism. It emphasised the physical skills and prowess of black people. The criticism, including by Sembène, is that this accepts the underpinning value of racism, that Europeans were technological, whilst Africans were spontaneous and non-technical. A character representing these views is criticised in "Camp de Thiaroye".



Neo-colonialism

The continuation of control over a country after independence. This is usually achieved by economic means, supported by political pressure, and occasionally military means. "Xala" opens with the celebrations of Independence in a new African State and quickly shows how foreign interests have suborned the local elite.



Neo-realism

A movement of Italian filmmakers in the late 1940s and early 1950s. the neo-realist films focussed on the lives and problems of ordinary people. For example, the theft of a worker's bicycle, essential for his job. ["Bicycle Thieves", 1948]. The films had a looser narrative than studio films, frequently used actual locations and non-professional actors, and relied on less sophisticated film technology. This approach, with less emphasis on high tech filmmaking and high production values offered a useful example to third world filmmakers. Some, like Fernando Birri [Argentina] or Julio García Espinosa [Cuba] studied in Rome and had direct contact with the movement. The Indian filmmakers we influenced when neo-realist films appeared at film Festivals there. Sembène's "Borom Sarret" is a good example of a film that shares a number of neo-realist attributes.
